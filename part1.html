The Hydrologic Cycle Drives the Whole Process

Part 1: The Basics of the Hydrologic Cycle

The hydrologic cycle, also known as the water cycle, is a continuous process that describes how water moves through a series of interconnected stages. This section breaks down the key components of this vital global system.

Evaporation and Transpiration

The cycle begins with evaporation, the process by which liquid water on the Earth's surface—from oceans, lakes, and rivers—is converted into water vapor and rises into the atmosphere. This process is driven primarily by solar energy. A related process, transpiration, is the release of water vapor from plants through their leaves. Combined, these two processes are often referred to as evapotranspiration.

Condensation and Precipitation

As the water vapor rises into the cooler atmosphere, it undergoes condensation, transforming from a gas back into a liquid state. This is a critical step in cloud formation, as the water droplets or ice crystals cluster around tiny particles in the air. When these droplets or crystals become too heavy to remain suspended, they fall back to Earth as precipitation in various forms, including rain, snow, sleet, or hail.

Runoff and Infiltration

Once precipitation reaches the ground, it follows one of two primary paths. It can become surface runoff, flowing over the land and eventually collecting in streams, rivers, lakes, and oceans. Alternatively, it can soak into the ground in a process called infiltration. This water can then be stored as groundwater or continue to move beneath the surface, feeding springs and contributing to the flow of rivers.

A Local Example: The Prairie Water Cycle

In West Central Saskatchewan during the peak of the crop growing season, the hydrologic cycle is a tangible process. The intense summer sun drives rapid evaporation from the numerous sloughs and ponds, while vast fields of wheat and canola contribute significant water vapor through transpiration. This moisture-rich air rises and undergoes condensation, forming the classic cumulus clouds that dot the prairie sky. These clouds often lead to localized thunderstorms, providing crucial precipitation for the crops. The rain falls onto the land, and the fertile, well-tilled soil absorbs a great deal through infiltration. Any excess water may become surface runoff, collecting in the low-lying areas and feeding the cycle once again.
