Early inhabitants of Canada depended on their knowledge of the environment for survival. They needed careful observations of wildlife habits and weather patterns. Today, while we are often detached from nature in our daily lives, systematic scientific observation remains crucial for understanding ecosystems and managing human impacts.

1. The Nature of Science

Science is a systematic process used to understand the world and universe. It involves asking questions, conducting orderly observations, and analyzing findings. While science focuses on rigorous observation, it is important to remember that it is not value-free; scientists have beliefs and backgrounds that influence data interpretation.

[Image of steps of the scientific method diagram]

Five Basic Assumptions of Science

When studying the natural world, scientists operate under five core assumptions:

  1. We can understand patterns in the natural world through observation.
  2. Science is based on inductive reasoning (generalizing from specific observations).
  3. Scientific rules apply throughout the universe.
  4. Generalizations can be tested and potentially disproved.
  5. Science never provides absolute proof; theories can always be modified by new evidence.
Reasoning in Science

Deductive Reasoning: Drawing conclusions from logical assumptions (general to specific).
Example: All soils with pH < 5.5 cannot support plants. This soil is pH 4.0. Therefore, this soil cannot support plants.

Inductive Reasoning: Drawing a conclusion based on limited observations (specific to general).
Example: I measured the temperature for 5 years and it was ~12°C. Therefore, next year it will be ~12°C.

Counter Example: An observation that disproves a hypothesis.
Example: If a scientist claims pH 4.0 soil cannot support plants (deductive), but finds a plant growing in it, that plant is a "counter example."

Scientific Disciplines in Environmental Science

Environmental Science is interdisciplinary. Here is how other fields contribute:

  • Chemistry: Understanding pollution, chemical cycles, and soil pH.
  • Biology: Studying ecosystems, species interactions, and biodiversity.
  • Statistics: Analyzing trends in population data and experimental results.
  • Ecology: Examining the relationships between organisms and their physical environment.
  • Meteorology: Understanding weather patterns, climate change, and atmospheric conditions.

2. Worldviews and Environmental Ethics

A Worldview is a set of commonly shared values and ideas concerning reality and humanity's role within it. In Western society, two major worldviews compete: the Expansionist and the Ecological.

Expansionist Worldview (Enlightenment) Ecological Worldview (Romantic/Counter-Enlightenment)
Nature is a resource to be used, not preserved. Nature is intrinsically valuable; biotic communities must be protected.
Conservation = "Wise Management" or sustainable exploitation. Preservation = "Righteous Management" and protecting systems.
Faith in science/technology to solve problems. Human activities must work within ecosystem limitations.

Justifications for Conservation

  • Utilitarian: The environment provides economic benefits or survival necessities (e.g., fisheries for jobs).
  • Ecological: Ecosystems provide functions necessary for life (e.g., forests removing CO2).
  • Aesthetic: Nature is beautiful and offers psychological restoration. ("Beauty is of profound importance").
  • Moral: Elements of the environment have a "right to exist" independent of human desire.
The Waves of Environmentalism

First Wave (1968-1976): Focused on pollution and protection. It ended largely due to Economics (inflation and economic trouble took priority).

Second Wave (1985-Present): Re-emerged with a focus on global issues (global warming, ozone) and "Sustainable Development."

3. Societal Development Stages

Human impact on the environment has changed as our societies have evolved through four main stages:

  1. Hunters and Gatherers: Low population density, minimal environmental impact.
  2. Pre-industrial Agriculture: Domestication of plants and animals began. Population density increased slightly.
  3. The Machine Age (Industrial Revolution): Rapid population growth due to medical/sanitation advances. Massive resource use.
  4. The Modern Era: High urbanization and technology. Canada is currently in this stage.

4. Human Population Dynamics

Demographic Terms
  • Natality: Births (Increases population)
  • Mortality: Deaths (Decreases population)
  • Immigration: Moving IN (Increases population)
  • Emigration: Moving OUT (Decreases population)
  • Crude Birth Rate: Number of births per 1,000 people.
  • Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Average number of children a woman will have in her lifetime.

Population Growth = (Natality + Immigration) - (Mortality + Emigration)

The Demographic Transition Model (DTM)

The Demographic Transition Model (DTM) describes four stages of economic development that nations tend to pass through, which correlates directly to changes in birth and death rates:

  1. Pre-industrial Stage: High birth rate and high death rate, resulting in little population growth.
  2. Transitional Stage: Death rates drop rapidly (due to improved sanitation/health care), but birth rates remain high. This causes the fastest rate of population growth.
  3. Industrial Stage: Birth rates begin to fall and approach the death rate as education and modernization increase. Population growth continues, but slows down.
  4. Post-industrial Stage: Birth rate falls further, equaling or falling below the death rate, leading to zero or even negative population growth (population decline).

As countries develop, they go through these 4 stages. A Demographic Trap occurs when a country gets stuck in Stage 2—death rates fall (due to better health), but birth rates stay high, leading to explosive population growth that overwhelms the economy.

Interpreting Population Pyramids

The shape of a population pyramid tells a story about a country's past, present, and future. By observing the width of the base (young people) versus the top (elderly), we can infer the population's stability and stage of development.

1. Youthful Population (Expanding)

Shape: Wide base, narrow top (Triangle).

Inference: High birth rate and high mortality rate. The population is young and growing rapidly.

Common in developing nations (Stage 2/3) where children are often needed for labor.

2. Ageing Population (Stable)

Shape: Rectangular or "Beehive" shape with vertical sides.

Inference: Birth rates and death rates are balanced. The population size is stable.

Typical of developed nations (Stage 4) with good healthcare and education.

3. Aged Population (Declining)

Shape: Narrow base, wider middle/top (Inverted/Cup).

Inference: Low birth rate that has fallen below replacement level. The population is shrinking and aging.

Seen in countries like Japan or Germany (Stage 5) where the workforce may shrink.

5. Solutions & Future Outlook

To reduce TFR (Total Fertility Rate) and stabilize population, nations typically focus on:

  • Education: Especially for women, which leads to delayed marriage and careers.
  • Health: Lowering infant mortality so parents don't feel the need to have "extra" children.
  • Contraception: Making family planning freely available.
Country Strategies

China: Used strict regulation (One Child Policy, fines). Rapid reduction.

India: Education and bureaucracy. Slower reduction.

Iran: Successfully used education, contraception, and religion to reduce TFR drastically.

Millennium Development Goals

Four specific goals help reduce TFR:

  1. Achieve universal primary education.
  2. Promote gender equality (empower women).
  3. Reduce child mortality.
  4. Improve maternal health.
