I. Unit Opener: Contextual Reading

The Haze on the Horizon: A Saskatchewan Summer

For the Moroz family in Prince Albert, the summer of 2023 began with the promise of camping trips to Waskesiu and long evenings in the backyard. But by mid-July, the vibrant blue of the "Land of Living Skies" had turned a sickly, opaque orange. A thick, acrid haze settled over the North Saskatchewan River valley, erasing the horizon.

"It smells like a campfire that won't go out," 10-year-old Leo said, pressing his nose against the living room window. Outside, the streetlights flickered on at 4:00 PM, tricked by the premature darkness.

His mother, Sarah, checked the app on her phone. The Air Quality Health Index (AQHI) was reading a "10+"—Very High Risk. "No soccer practice today, Leo," she sighed, closing the blinds. The particulate matter from massive forest fires burning hundreds of kilometers north in the Boreal Shield was drifting south, trapped by a stagnant high-pressure weather system. For Leo, who managed mild asthma, the invisible particles in that haze were more than just an annoyance; they were a physical danger.

As Sarah turned on their HEPA air purifier, a thought crossed her mind: Is this just a bad summer, or is this our new normal? This unit explores the systems that created the Moroz family's predicament—from the chemistry of the air we breathe to the global climate trends shaping our local weather, and the technologies we are developing here in Saskatchewan to steward our future.

II. Section 1: The Air We Breathe (ES20-AH1)

The atmosphere is not merely empty space; it is a dynamic ocean of gases that protects and sustains life on Earth. While its composition has remained relatively stable for millennia, human activity—specifically the burning of fossil fuels and industrial processes—is altering this delicate balance.

A. Composition and Structure of the Atmosphere

Before understanding pollution, we must understand the baseline. The Earth's atmosphere is a mixture of gases held in place by gravity.

1. Major Gases

The "air" you breathe is not just oxygen. It is actually a mixture dominated by nitrogen.

  • Nitrogen (N2) - 78%: The most abundant gas. It is relatively inert (unreactive) and essential for plant growth (via nitrogen-fixing bacteria in soil).
  • Oxygen (O2) - 21%: Essential for respiration in animals and combustion processes.
  • Argon (Ar) - 0.9%: A noble gas that does not react with other substances.
  • Trace Gases - 0.1%: This tiny fraction includes carbon dioxide (CO2), neon, helium, methane, and krypton. despite their small percentage, gases like CO2 and methane drive the greenhouse effect.
  • Water Vapor (H2O): Varies from 0% (in deserts) to 4% (in humid tropics). It is the most abundant greenhouse gas and the driver of weather.

2. Layers of the Atmosphere

The atmosphere is divided into five distinct layers based on temperature trends. Each layer plays a unique role in protecting the planet.

  • Troposphere (Surface to ~12 km): This is where we live. It contains 75% of the atmosphere's mass and almost all water vapor. This is where weather occurs. In this layer, temperature decreases as you go higher (which is why mountains have snow).
  • Stratosphere (12 km to 50 km): Home to the Ozone Layer. Unlike the troposphere, this layer gets warmer as you go up because the ozone absorbs UV radiation. It is very stable, which is why commercial jets fly here to avoid turbulence.
  • Mesosphere (50 km to 85 km): The coldest layer (dropping to -90°C). It protects Earth by burning up most meteors upon entry, creating "shooting stars."
  • Thermosphere (85 km to 600 km): A layer of extremely thin air where temperatures can soar to 2,000°C due to solar radiation, though it would feel cold to human skin because the molecules are so far apart.
    Saskatchewan Context: This is where the Aurora Borealis (Northern Lights) occurs, caused by charged solar particles colliding with atmospheric gases.
  • Exosphere (600 km+): The outermost layer where the atmosphere fades into the vacuum of space. Satellites orbit here.

Science 10 Review: The Atmosphere & Reactions

Recall from Science 10 that our atmosphere is stratified into layers based on temperature trends. We live in the troposphere (0-12 km), the dense lower layer where all weather occurs. Above that lies the stratosphere, home to the ozone layer which shields us from UV radiation.

You also learned about combustion reactions. Ideally, burning a hydrocarbon produces only carbon dioxide and water:
Hydrocarbon + O2 → CO2 + H2O + Energy.

However, in the real world, oxygen is often limited. This leads to incomplete combustion, releasing harmful byproducts like carbon monoxide (CO) and solid carbon (soot).

B. Outdoor Air Quality

Air pollution is generally categorized into two types. Primary pollutants are emitted directly from a source, such as soot from a diesel truck or sulfur dioxide from a volcano. Secondary pollutants form when primary pollutants react chemically in the atmosphere. The most common example is ground-level ozone (smog), created when sunlight triggers reactions between nitrogen oxides and volatile organic compounds.

In Saskatchewan, our air quality challenges are uniquely tied to our geography and economy:

  • Wildfire Smoke: As seen in the opening story, fires in the northern Boreal forest release massive amounts of Particulate Matter 2.5 (PM2.5). These microscopic particles are less than 2.5 microns in diameter—about 3% the width of a human hair. Unlike larger dust particles, PM2.5 can bypass the body's natural defenses (nose hair and mucus) and travel deep into the lungs and bloodstream.
  • Agriculture: During seeding and harvest, the air in rural Saskatchewan often carries dust (PM10). Additionally, livestock operations can release ammonia and methane, which contribute to local air quality issues.
  • Industry & Sour Gas: In Southeast Saskatchewan's oilfields, a major concern is "Sour Gas"—natural gas containing Hydrogen Sulfide (H2S). H2S is highly toxic; at low concentrations, it smells like rotten eggs, but at high concentrations, it deadens the sense of smell and can be instantly fatal.

Exploring Further: The Inversion Effect

Why does smog sometimes "sit" over a city or valley for days? This phenomenon is often caused by a temperature inversion.

Normally, air gets colder as you go higher. Warm air near the ground rises, dispersing pollutants into the upper atmosphere. However, during calm winter nights in places like the Qu'Appelle Valley, the ground cools rapidly, creating a layer of cold, dense air trapped beneath a layer of warmer air above.

This "lid" prevents vertical mixing, trapping vehicle exhaust and chimney smoke near the ground, causing air quality to plummet until the wind picks up.

C. The Ozone Layer: Protection vs. Pollution

It is vital to distinguish between the "bad" ozone found in smog (ground-level) and the "good" ozone found in the stratosphere. While ground-level ozone damages our lungs, stratospheric ozone acts as Earth's natural sunscreen.

Stratospheric Ozone Depletion and Human Health:
Certain human-made chemicals, particularly CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons) once used in refrigeration, have migrated to the stratosphere and degraded this protective layer. This thinning, often called the "ozone hole," allows more Ultraviolet B (UV-B) radiation to reach the Earth's surface.

The impact of increased UV-B radiation on human health is significant:

  • Skin Cancer: UV radiation damages DNA in skin cells. This damage accumulates over time, leading to higher rates of carcinomas and melanomas, particularly in people with lighter skin.
  • Cataracts: UV-B radiation oxidizes the proteins in the lens of the eye. Over time, this causes the lens to cloud over, a condition known as cataracts, which is a leading cause of blindness globally.

Critical Misconception: Ozone vs. Climate Change

True or False: The hole in the ozone layer lets in more heat, causing global warming.

FALSE. This is one of the most common misunderstandings in environmental science.

  • Ozone Depletion is about UV Radiation. It causes sunburns, cancer, and cataracts, but UV rays carry very little heat energy.
  • Global Warming is about Infrared Heat. Greenhouse gases (like CO2) act like a blanket, trapping heat radiating from the Earth.

Think of it this way: The Ozone layer is like sunglasses (protecting eyes). Greenhouse gases are like a heavy winter coat (trapping body heat). Wearing broken sunglasses won't make you overheat, but it will hurt your eyes. Wearing a heavy coat in summer will make you overheat.

D. Indoor Air Quality: The Silent Hazard

While we often focus on outdoor smog, the air inside our homes can be up to five times more polluted. In Saskatchewan, the most significant indoor threat is geological.

Radon is a colorless, odorless, radioactive gas produced by the natural decay of uranium in soil and rock. Because Saskatchewan's geology is uranium-rich, our potential for radon exposure is among the highest in the world. Radon enters homes through the "stack effect": warm air rising inside the house creates a vacuum at the lowest level, sucking soil gases in through cracks in the foundation, sump pump pits, and gaps around pipes.

Long-term exposure to radon is the second leading cause of lung cancer in Canada. Health Canada estimates that approximately 16% of Saskatchewan homes exceed the safety guideline of 200 Becquerels per cubic metre (Bq/m³). The only way to detect it is through a long-term test kit, usually placed in the basement for 90 days during winter.

E. Regulations and Mitigation

To protect public health, the provincial government enforces the Saskatchewan Environmental Management and Protection Act. This legislation limits the volume of pollutants facilities can emit. Industries use sophisticated technologies to meet these standards:

  • Electrostatic Precipitators: These massive devices are used in industrial smokestacks, such as those at coal-fired power plants. They work like "static cling" on an industrial scale. As exhaust gas passes through, high-voltage wires impart a negative charge to dust particles. These charged particles are then attracted to positively charged collection plates, removing up to 99% of particulate matter before it leaves the stack.
  • Scrubbers: To combat acid rain, "wet scrubbers" spray a slurry of limestone and water through exhaust gases. The limestone reacts with sulfur dioxide (SO2) to form synthetic gypsum (calcium sulfate), which can be recycled into drywall.

III. Section 2: Analyzing a Changing Climate (ES20-AH2)

While "weather" describes the state of the atmosphere at a specific time and place (e.g., a rainy Tuesday in Saskatoon), climate refers to the long-term averages of weather patterns over decades or centuries. Understanding climate requires analyzing data that spans far beyond human history.

Science 10 Review: The Greenhouse Effect

The Earth remains habitable due to the Greenhouse Effect. The sun sends high-energy shortwave radiation to Earth, which passes easily through the atmosphere. The Earth absorbs this energy and re-radiates it as lower-energy longwave (infrared) heat.

Greenhouse gases (GHGs) like Carbon Dioxide (CO2), Methane (CH4), and Water Vapor act like a blanket: they let the shortwave energy in but trap the longwave heat trying to escape. This natural process is good—without it, Earth would be a frozen rock. The problem is the Anthropogenic Greenhouse Effect: humans are adding too many "blankets," causing the planet to overheat.

B. Geoscience Data: How Do We Know?

Since reliable thermometer records only go back about 150 years, scientists rely on proxy data—preserved physical characteristics of the environment—to reconstruct past climates.

  • Ice Cores: In Antarctica and Greenland, snow has fallen for hundreds of thousands of years without melting, compressing into distinct layers of ice. By drilling deep cores, scientists can analyze tiny air bubbles trapped in the ice. these bubbles are literal samples of the atmosphere from 800,000 years ago, allowing us to measure exact CO2 levels from the past.
  • Dendrochronology (Tree Rings): Trees in temperate climates grow a new ring every year. In warm, wet years, the ring is wide; in cold, dry years, it is narrow. By analyzing old-growth trees in Northern Saskatchewan, researchers can create a "barcode" of climate history, revealing patterns of drought and plenty that date back centuries.

C. Impacts on Saskatchewan

Climate models predict significant shifts for our province, which will require adaptation:

  • Shifting Hardiness Zones: As average temperatures rise, the growing season is lengthening. While this may allow farmers to grow crops like corn and soybeans further north, it also disrupts established ecosystems.
  • Water Security: The North and South Saskatchewan Rivers differ from other prairie waterways—they are fed largely by glacial melt from the Rocky Mountains. This glacial water is a crucial "savings account" that keeps the rivers flowing during hot, dry summers. As glaciers shrink, this late-summer flow is at risk, threatening irrigation and drinking water for cities like Saskatoon and Regina.
  • Pests and Invasive Species: The Mountain Pine Beetle is a devastating pest that kills pine trees. Historically, Saskatchewan's bitter winters (specifically, extended periods below -40°C) killed the beetle larvae, keeping them out of the province. Warmer winters have allowed the beetle to survive and spread eastward from Alberta, threatening the Jack Pine forests of the Canadian Shield.

IV. Section 3: Population, Pollution, and Solutions (ES20-HP1)

As the global human population grows, so does our consumption of resources and production of waste. Sustainable living requires a fundamental shift in how we manage our materials.

Science 10 Review: Carrying Capacity

Recall the concept of Carrying Capacity—the maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely. In nature, populations that exceed their carrying capacity usually crash due to starvation or disease. Humans have used technology (agriculture, medicine) to artificially raise Earth's carrying capacity, but this comes at an environmental cost.

B. The Waste Problem: Linear vs. Circular

Our current economy is largely linear: we extract resources, make products, and dispose of them ("Take-Make-Waste"). A circular economy aims to keep resources in use for as long as possible through recycling and reusing.

In Saskatchewan, managing waste is complicated by our low population density. Transporting recyclables from remote rural communities to processing centers is often expensive and carbon-intensive. Modern landfills are sophisticated engineering projects designed to protect the environment. They are lined with thick clay and plastic to catch leachate—a toxic "garbage juice" formed when rainwater filters through waste. Pipes are also installed to capture methane gas produced by decomposing organic matter, which can sometimes be burned to generate electricity.

C. Water and Resource Management

Urban centers produce massive amounts of wastewater. If released untreated, human waste adds excess nitrogen and phosphorus to rivers. This causes eutrophication: an explosion of algae growth that depletes oxygen in the water, killing fish.

The City of Saskatoon uses an advanced process called Biological Nutrient Removal (BNR). Instead of using harsh chemicals, the treatment plant uses bacteria to clean the water.
1. In oxygen-rich zones, Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter bacteria convert ammonia (from urine) into nitrate.
2. The water is then moved to an oxygen-starved (anoxic) zone. Here, different bacteria (Pseudomonas) are forced to strip the oxygen atoms off the nitrate molecules to survive.
3. This process converts the nitrate into harmless nitrogen gas, which bubbles out into the atmosphere, leaving the water clean enough to return to the river.

D. Mitigation Technologies: Carbon Capture

Saskatchewan is a world leader in Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS). The Boundary Dam project near Estevan was the world's first commercial-scale CCS facility attached to a coal-fired power plant.

The process works by diverting exhaust smoke into a tower filled with an amine solution. The amine binds to the CO2 molecules, separating them from the rest of the exhaust. The captured CO2 is then compressed into a liquid-like state and pumped 3.4 kilometers deep underground into the Aquistore injection site. There, it is trapped in a layer of porous rock (the Deadwood formation) beneath a cap of impermeable shale, preventing it from contributing to climate change.

Exploring Further: Alternative Energy

While CCS manages fossil fuel emissions, Saskatchewan also has immense potential for renewable energy. The Cowessess First Nation, located east of Regina, has developed a groundbreaking hybrid energy project.

Their facility combines an 800kW wind turbine with over 2,000 solar panels and a battery storage system. This "hybrid" approach solves the intermittency problem: the batteries store excess power when the sun shines or wind blows, releasing it when it's calm and dark. This project demonstrates how Indigenous leadership and technology can create sustainable, sovereign power solutions.

V. Chapter Review & Assessment

Summary of Key Terms

  • AQHI: Air Quality Health Index (1-10+ scale) measuring health risk from pollution.
  • Radon: Radioactive gas found in basements; enters via stack effect; #2 cause of lung cancer.
  • Proxy Data: Indirect evidence (ice cores, tree rings) used to infer past climate conditions.
  • Albedo: The reflectivity of a surface. (Ice = High Albedo; Water = Low Albedo).
  • Leachate: Liquid pollution draining from a landfill that must be contained.
  • BNR: Biological Nutrient Removal; using bacteria to clean wastewater.
  • CCS: Carbon Capture and Storage technology.

VI. Appendices & Teacher Resources

Lab Activity 1: "Mining" for Data

Objective: To simulate how ice cores record atmospheric history.

Procedure: Over the course of a week, freeze successive layers of water in a clear graduated cylinder. In each layer, add different "proxies": a drop of red food coloring (volcanic eruption), a pinch of ash (forest fire), or a layer of bubbly soda water (high CO2 concentration). Have students "drill" a core using a clear straw and analyze the timeline of the "atmosphere" based on the depth of the layers.

Appendix A: Indigenous Ways of Knowing

Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) is the cumulative body of knowledge, practice, and belief evolving by adaptive processes and handed down through generations. In the context of weather and climate:

  • Reading the Land: Indigenous peoples in Saskatchewan have observed weather patterns for millennia. For example, observing the height of muskrat lodges can predict the severity of spring runoff. Higher lodges suggest the animals anticipate higher water levels.
  • Stewardship vs. Management: Western science often focuses on "managing" resources (e.g., controlling river flow). Indigenous perspectives often emphasize "stewardship" and relationship—treating the air and water as relatives to be respected rather than commodities to be used. This shift in mindset is crucial for sustainable decision-making.
