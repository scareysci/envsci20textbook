Section 2: The Producers: Plants, Ecosystems Services, and Human Reliance (ES20-TE2)

Plants are the primary producers in almost all terrestrial ecosystems. They capture solar energy through photosynthesis, forming the energy base for the entire food web. Curriculum Outcome ES20-TE2 asks us to examine the critical role plants play, including the ways in which humans use them for economic and cultural benefit.

2.1 Photosynthesis: The Engine of Terrestrial Life

Photosynthesis is arguably the most vital chemical process on Earth. It is the complex mechanism by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy from the sun into chemical energy stored in sugar (glucose). This process forms the basis of all food webs and regulates the planet’s atmosphere (Häder & Sinha, 2022).

Inputs and Outputs:

  • Inputs (Reactants): The plant requires Carbon Dioxide (CO2) from the atmosphere and Water (H2O) absorbed from the soil, along with Light Energy captured by the chlorophyll pigment.
  • Outputs (Products): The major products are Glucose (C6H12O6), a sugar used by the plant for energy and growth, and Oxygen Gas (O2), which is released into the atmosphere. This removal of atmospheric CO2 is why plants are essential carbon sinks, mitigating climate change.

The Balanced Chemical Equation (Simplified):

6CO2 + 6H2O → C6H12O6 + 6O2
Catalysts: Sunlight Energy, Chlorophyll

This simplified equation illustrates the conservation of matter, converting inorganic matter into organic fuel and breathable oxygen (Stirbet et al., 2019).

2.2 Soil Stabilization: The Anchor

The most visible physical function of terrestrial vegetation is anchoring the soil. Plant root systems act like steel rebar in concrete, mechanically reinforcing the soil matrix and dramatically increasing the soil's shear strength (Cao et al., 2023; Mairaing et al., 2024). This composite structure is crucial for resisting the erosive forces of wind and water, especially in fragile environments like the prairies.

  • Above-Ground Protection: The plant's canopy, stalks, and leaf litter break the force of falling raindrops, preventing them from detaching soil particles. They also slow down surface runoff, giving water more time to infiltrate (Derpsch, 2015).
  • Below-Ground Cohesion: Roots bind fine mineral particles together, often with the help of sticky compounds produced by soil microorganisms, forming stable soil aggregates. This cohesion makes the soil heavier and far less likely to be blown away by wind or washed away by water.

Biology in Action: Shelterbelts and Agroforestry

Utilizing plants to create wind barriers and prevent the loss of topsoil.

In the open Grasslands and Parkland regions, shelterbelts (rows of trees planted strategically along fields) utilize plant structure to protect crops. These barriers reduce wind speed across the soil surface, minimizing wind erosion which was devastating during the Dust Bowl era. Additionally, agroforestry—integrating trees or shrubs with crop fields (like alley cropping or riparian buffers)—stabilizes field margins and reduces the movement of sediment off the land and into waterways (IRJEdT, 2023).

2.3 Water Regulation: Nature’s Sponge

Vegetation is integral to the local hydrologic cycle by mediating how water enters and leaves the soil system. It helps the land act as a giant, rechargeable sponge.

  • Enhanced Infiltration: As plant roots grow and then naturally decompose, they create microscopic vertical channels known as biopores (Dagenais et al., 2018). These pores act as highly efficient drainage pathways, allowing rainwater to quickly penetrate deep into the soil profile rather than pooling and running off.
  • Drought Resistance: By improving infiltration and adding organic matter, plants increase the soil's ability to retain water. This retained water is crucial during dry periods, making the ecosystem, whether it's a native prairie or an agricultural field, far more resilient to drought (Basche & Edelson, 2017).
  • Groundwater Recharge: By facilitating deep infiltration, plants help guide water past the root zone, contributing directly to the replenishment of groundwater reserves.

Biology in Action: No-Till and Continuous Living Cover

Managing the field surface to maximize water use efficiency.

Modern conservation agriculture utilizes plants through no-till farming combined with cover crops. By leaving crop residue on the surface (permanent soil cover) and planting roots that grow continuously throughout the year (continuous living cover), farmers maximize the ecological benefits of plants (Derpsch, 2015). This system minimizes disruption to the natural biopores, maximizes the trapping of snow water in winter (Baulch et al., 2019), and prevents runoff, ensuring that precious precipitation is conserved in the soil for the next growing season, which is essential in semi-arid Saskatchewan.

2.4 Human Reliance on Terrestrial Plant Resources

2.4.1 Food and Fiber (Agriculture)

Feeding a growing global population requires Sustainable Intensification, which means maximizing food production on existing farmland while minimizing environmental harm (Jain et al., 2023). Saskatchewan farmers are leaders in this field, growing crops that not only provide food and fiber (like canola) but also actively improve the ecosystem services of their land.

This reliance demands careful planning to reduce the environmental footprint, specifically by optimizing the use of synthetic fertilizers and water.

Biology in Action: Pulse Crops and Precision Farming

Using technology and natural systems to boost efficiency and sustainability.

  • Pulse Crops: The rotation of nitrogen-fixing crops like lentils and dry peas (pulses) enriches the soil. These plants host bacteria that capture atmospheric nitrogen, naturally fertilizing the soil for the next year’s crop and reducing the reliance on synthetic nitrogen fertilizer by up to 37% (Lika et al., 2024).
  • Precision Agriculture: Farmers use remote sensing (e.g., LiDAR, satellite imagery) and GPS to map fields and identify areas of high or low productivity. This data-driven approach allows for the highly precise application of fertilizer and pesticides, ensuring inputs are only used where needed. This maximizes profit while minimizing nutrient runoff into waterways (Farhan et al., 2024).
2.4.2 Timber and Forestry

The Boreal Forest in Northern Saskatchewan is a major economic engine, providing wood for lumber, pulp, and paper. The challenge in this sector is balancing the economic need for timber with the ecological need to maintain a healthy, diverse forest ecosystem that provides intact habitat and stores carbon.

Unsustainable harvesting can lead to habitat homogenization—where the forest becomes uniform in structure and age—which reduces biodiversity and increases vulnerability to fire and disease (Montoro Girona et al., 2019).

Biology in Action: Selective vs. Clear-Cutting

Choosing harvest methods to maintain ecological structure.

  • Clear-Cutting: While efficient, this practice removes almost all standing timber, which can dramatically reduce the amount of coarse woody debris (dead logs and branches) needed for nutrient cycling and animal habitat (Alissa, 1999). It also increases windthrow (trees blown over) on nearby edges (Montoro Girona et al., 2019).
  • Partial Cutting: Sustainable practices often utilize partial cutting (or shelterwood systems) where only certain trees are removed. This aims to mimic smaller, natural disturbances, maintaining a multi-aged forest structure. It retains better habitat, protects the remaining trees from wind damage, and ensures a supply of seeds for natural regeneration (Martin et al., 2021).
2.4.3 Medicines and Traditional Uses

Beyond the large-scale industries of farming and forestry, plants provide essential Non-Timber Forest Products (NTFPs) that hold high cultural and local economic value. These include wild rice, berries, medicinal barks, and ceremonial plants like Sweetgrass.

For Indigenous communities in Saskatchewan, the relationship with these plants is governed by Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK). This knowledge emphasizes a relationship of reciprocity and kinship with the land, viewing plants as relatives rather than simple resources (Mehltretter et al., 2024).

Biology in Action: Sustaining Manoomin (Wild Rice)

Integrating culture and conservation for traditional foods.

Wild Rice (*Zizania palustris*), or Manoomin, is a vital aquatic plant harvested traditionally across Northern Saskatchewan. Historically, Indigenous women often held the rights and responsibility for managing the harvest of Manoomin, ensuring that harvesting methods (like knocking seeds with sticks) only take a portion of the seed crop (Ferreira et al., 2022). This method acts as an annual ecosystem service, reseeding the beds for future growth and ensuring the health of the water ecosystem. This practice demonstrates a sustainable harvest protocol based on deep ecological understanding.

2.4.4 Monoculture vs. Grassland: Lessons in Diversity

The conversion of native perennial grasslands into fields of annual crop monocultures (like continuous wheat or canola) is the primary land-use change in the prairies. While monocultures are efficient for large-scale production, they disrupt key ecological processes that natural grasslands provide (Crews et al., 2018).

Natural grasslands exhibit high species and functional diversity (grasses, legumes, forbs) and maintain roots year-round. This diversity increases the system's resilience—its ability to bounce back from disturbance—and enhances soil health and water retention far better than a simple annual crop (Lin, 2011; Thompson & Kao-Kniffin, 2016).

Biology in Action: Mimicking Grassland Complexity

Agricultural strategies designed to restore ecological services.

  • Complex Crop Rotation: Moving beyond simple two-crop rotations (e.g., wheat-fallow) to multi-species rotations that include functionally diverse crops (legumes, cereals, oilseeds) enhances nutrient cycling, improves soil carbon storage, and reduces pest and weed pressure over time (Smith et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2024).
  • Intercropping: This involves planting two or more compatible crops in the same field simultaneously (e.g., mixing a cereal grass with a legume). This mimics the spatial diversity of a natural plant community, leading to better resource use efficiency and higher total yields, especially under low nitrogen conditions (Perrot et al., 2023; Smith et al., 2023).
  • Perennialization: The ultimate goal is creating perennial grain crops (crops that live for multiple years and don't need annual tillage) to match the continuous soil cover provided by native grasses, drastically reducing erosion and increasing Soil Organic Matter (Crews et al., 2018).

2.5 Spotlight: Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) and Indigenous Land Stewardship

Indigenous worldviews often emphasize the concept of reciprocity—a relationship where humans take only what they need and actively give back to the land, viewing themselves as part of the ecosystem, not separate from it.

Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) is the cumulative body of knowledge, practice, and belief, evolving by adaptive processes and handed down through generations, relating to the relationship of living beings (including humans) with their environment. TEK often includes practices such as:

  • Cultural Burning: Historically used in some regions to clear old brush, reduce dangerous fuel loads, and stimulate the growth of valuable forage plants, promoting ecosystem health and resilience.
  • Seasonal Rotation: Moving camps and harvesting areas to allow ecosystems time to recover, ensuring resources are available for future generations.

These practices embody a deep understanding of local ecology that is increasingly being integrated into modern environmental management plans across Canada.
