Chapter 1 — The Chemistry of Life

Life depends on the structure and behavior of chemical substances. In this chapter, you will connect core chemistry ideas from Science 10 to biological macromolecules, membranes, and enzyme‑mediated reactions that power cells—directly aligning to Saskatchewan’s BI30‑OL1 outcome (cell structure & processes, energy transfer, and material transport).[SK]

Saskatchewan Context & Outcome Alignment

This chapter meets BI30‑OL1: “Investigate cell structure and processes, including energy transfer and transport of materials, in unicellular and multicellular organisms…” and prepares you for Genetics (GB), Evolution (LE), and Diversity (OL2/OL3) units.[SK] For scope/depth alignment across provinces, major concepts mirror Alberta Biology 30 Unit C (Genetics & Molecular Biology) foundations and BC Anatomy & Physiology 12 biochemistry/cell structure topics.[AB] [BC]

1.1 Recall — Atoms, Ions, and Reactions

Matter is composed of atoms, which form molecules via chemical bonds. In living systems, covalent bonds create strong frameworks (e.g., peptide bonds in proteins), while hydrogen bonds provide flexible interactions (e.g., between water molecules and within DNA base pairs). Reaction rates depend on temperature, concentration, surface area, and catalysts—principles you explored in Science 10 and will now see in biological contexts.[SK] [BC]

Deep Dive ▸ Polar vs. Nonpolar Molecules

Water’s polarity enables solvent action, cohesion, and moderation of temperature—vital for prairie and boreal organisms. Nonpolar lipids cluster to form membranes, creating compartments that support life’s chemistry.[BC]

1.2 Biological Macromolecules

Four macromolecule families underpin cell structure and metabolism: carbohydrates (energy & structural polysaccharides), lipids (membranes & long‑term energy), proteins (enzymes & cellular machinery), and nucleic acids (information storage & expression). Their structures determine function, and small variations can produce major physiological differences across Saskatchewan species and crops.[SK] [BC]

Carbohydrates

Monosaccharides (e.g., glucose) join to form disaccharides and polysaccharides. Plants synthesize starch for energy storage and cellulose for cell wall structure. In animals, glycogen serves as a rapid energy reserve in liver and muscle.

Lipids

Phospholipids form bilayers that create selectively permeable membranes. Unsaturated fatty acids (with double bonds) increase membrane fluidity at lower temperatures—an adaptive advantage for organisms in the Prairie Ecozone.[BC]

Proteins

Proteins are polymers of amino acids with complex 3D structures (primary→quaternary). They act as enzymes, transporters (e.g., aquaporins), receptors, and structural elements. Even small pH or temperature shifts can alter protein conformation and function.

Nucleic Acids

DNA stores genetic information; RNA participates in expression (mRNA, tRNA, rRNA). The complementary base‑pairing (A–T, C–G) and antiparallel strands enable replication and accurate information transfer—setting the stage for Unit 2: Molecular Genetics.[SK GB2]

1.3 Enzymes & Metabolic Reactions

Enzymes are biological catalysts that lower activation energy and increase reaction rate without being consumed. Their activity depends on substrate concentration, temperature, pH, and inhibitors. In Saskatchewan organisms, enzyme regulation supports survival across seasonal temperature ranges and in drought‑prone environments.[SK] [BC]

Explore more ▸ Factors Affecting Enzyme Action
  • Temperature: Increases kinetic energy up to an optimal point; excessive heat denatures protein.
  • pH: Alters active‑site charge and shape; each enzyme has an optimal pH range.
  • Substrate concentration: Rate rises until enzymes saturate (Vmax).
  • Inhibitors: Competitive vs noncompetitive, including environmental toxins.

Self‑check: Which change most likely reduces enzyme activity by altering the active site’s shape?





Lab Idea ▸ Catalase Rate Investigation

Purpose: Measure oxygen production from H2O2 decomposition with catalase under varying pH.

Method: Use yeast suspension or liver extract, record gas volume or bubble count vs. time at pH 5, 7, 9. Plot rate curves and analyze optimal pH.

Safety: Wear goggles/gloves; handle H2O2 carefully; dispose solutions per teacher guidance.

Saskatchewan Context: C3 vs C4 Plants

Prairie agriculture features predominantly C3 crops (e.g., wheat), while C4 crops (e.g., corn) have enzymes and leaf anatomy that reduce photorespiration in heat and high light. Differences in carbon fixation pathways and membrane fluidity adaptations influence growth and drought tolerance—connecting chemistry, enzymes, and physiology to real fields across the province.[SK] [BC]

Compare ▸ C3 vs C4 (quick reference)
  • C3 (Calvin cycle in mesophyll): More photorespiration under high heat/light; common in cooler climates.
  • C4 (Hatch–Slack pathway): CO2 pre‑fixation in mesophyll; Calvin cycle in bundle sheath cells; efficient at high temp/light.

Chapter Summary

  • Chemistry underlies biological structure and reactions; water’s polarity and hydrogen bonding are central to life.
  • Macromolecules (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids) enable membranes, metabolism, and information flow.
  • Enzymes lower activation energy and regulate reaction rates; their activity depends on temperature, pH, and inhibitors.
  • Local adaptations (e.g., C3 vs C4 plants) link molecular mechanisms to Saskatchewan’s agricultural and ecological contexts.
