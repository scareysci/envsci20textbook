Section 3: Biodiversity, Habitat Fragmentation, and Conservation (ES20-TE3)

3.1 The Need for Intact Habitat and Measuring Biodiversity

Curriculum Outcome ES20-TE3 focuses on recognizing the need for intact habitat to support animal populations and overall biodiversity. Biodiversity is the variety of life at three levels: genetic diversity, species diversity, and ecosystem diversity. A high level of biodiversity increases the resilience of an ecosystem—its ability to recover from disturbances like disease or climate change. In Saskatchewan, the rapid conversion of native prairie to agricultural land has been the primary driver of biodiversity decline.

3.2 Habitat Fragmentation and Edge Effects

Habitat Fragmentation occurs when large, continuous habitats are divided into smaller, isolated patches by roads, fields, or urban development.

  • Edge Effects: The boundary zones (edges) between fragmented habitats (e.g., between a forest patch and an agricultural field) experience different environmental conditions (more wind, sun, and predation) than the deep interior. This reduces the effective size of the habitat patch, negatively impacting species that need large, undisturbed areas.
  • Reduced Gene Flow: Fragmentation can prevent animal populations from mixing, leading to inbreeding and loss of genetic diversity, making the population more vulnerable to extinction.

Case Study: Prairie Species Under Pressure

  • The Burrowing Owl (Athene cunicularia): An endangered species relying entirely on native grasslands, nesting in burrows. Its decline is a direct indicator of habitat loss and fragmentation.
  • The Swift Fox (Vulpes velox): Once extirpated from Canada, this tiny fox relies on healthy, continuous short- and mixed-grass prairie. Reintroduction efforts depend heavily on managing land use and reducing human-caused mortality (like road deaths).
  • The Greater Sage Grouse (Centrocercus urophasianus): Highly sensitive to habitat disturbance, especially from human infrastructure (wells, roads). The male's elaborate courtship display requires large, open tracts of undisturbed land.

3.3 Conservation Strategies

Conservation efforts in Saskatchewan generally fall into two categories:

  1. In-situ Conservation (On-Site): Protecting species in their natural habitat. This includes creating Protected Areas (National Parks, Provincial Parks, Wildlife Refuges) and implementing Stewardship Agreements with landowners to protect critical habitats on private land.
  2. Ex-situ Conservation (Off-Site): Protecting species outside their natural habitat. This includes captive breeding programs (like those used for the Swift Fox) and seed banks, which store genetic material for future use;

Project Choice Menu: Terrestrial Systems

Choose one of the six options below to demonstrate your understanding of soil, biodiversity, and human interactions with Saskatchewan's terrestrial ecosystems.

Option 1: The Salinization Crisis: Mitigation Plan

(Soil Degradation - ES20-TE1 Focus)

Deliverable: Report & Model

Research a specific area in Southern Saskatchewan (e.g., Quill Lakes area) affected by salinization. Identify the hydrological cause, propose a three-part mitigation strategy (e.g., planting salt-tolerant cover crops, tile drainage), and explain the ecological benefit of your plan.

Outcomes: ES20-TE1, ES20-TE2

Option 2: Keystone Species Trophic Cascade Model

(Biodiversity & Habitat - ES20-TE3 Focus)

Deliverable: Presentation & Diagram

Choose a keystone species critical to a Saskatchewan ecosystem (e.g., Bison, Prairie Dogs, Wolves in the Boreal). Model the "trophic cascade" that would occur if that species was removed or reintroduced, detailing the specific impacts on plant diversity and soil health.

Outcomes: ES20-TE3, ES20-TE1

Option 3: Sustainable Resource Harvest Protocol

(Human Reliance & TEK - ES20-TE2 Focus)

Deliverable: Protocol Document

Develop a sustainable harvest plan for a non-timber forest product (e.g., wild rice, Sweetgrass, or berries) in a specific Saskatchewan region. The plan must integrate principles of Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) / Reciprocity and include a cost-benefit analysis.

Outcomes: ES20-TE2, ES20-ES1

Option 4: Habitat Corridor Design Challenge

(Fragmentation Mitigation - ES20-TE3 Focus)

Deliverable: Map & Rationale

Select two real-world, fragmented natural areas in the Aspen Parkland or Prairie region (use Google Maps coordinates). Design a functional wildlife corridor. Your rationale must explain how the corridor minimizes edge effects and promotes genetic flow for two specific target species (e.g., Moose, Swift Fox).

Outcomes: ES20-TE3

Option 5: Climate Change and Soil Carbon

(Systems Thinking - ES20-TE1/TE2 Link)

Deliverable: Research Essay

Research and analyze how predicted changes to Saskatchewan's climate (e.g., warmer winters, higher flood risk) will affect the ability of Chernozemic soils to act as a carbon sink. Discuss which land management practice (e.g., no-till farming, cover cropping) will be most essential to maintain soil carbon storage.

Outcomes: ES20-TE1, ES20-TE2

Option 6: The Local Land-Use Analysis

(Comprehensive - All Outcomes)

Deliverable: Field Study & Analysis

Investigate a specific local land-use issue (e.g., new subdivision, industrial site) in your community. Analyze the impact on soil, biodiversity, and resource use, and propose a sustainable alternative. This project synthesizes the entire unit.

Outcomes: ES20-TE1, ES20-TE2, ES20-TE3
