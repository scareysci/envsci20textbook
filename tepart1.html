Introduction: Life on the Ground and the Biomes of Saskatchewan

When we think of Environmental Science, we often focus on global issues. However, the study of the land beneath our feet—our terrestrial ecosystems—is where the vast majority of human resource management occurs, especially here in Saskatchewan. These ecosystems are organized into major life zones called biomes.

A biome is a massive geographical area characterized by similar climate, geology, and typical plant and animal life. They are primarily determined by two abiotic factors: temperature and precipitation. Because Saskatchewan spans a wide range of latitude and climate zones, our province contains three major terrestrial biomes, each with unique ecological challenges and human uses.

Key Characteristics of Saskatchewan's Major Biomes

Biome/Ecoregion Location & Extent Key Biotic Characteristics Key Abiotic Characteristics
Boreal Forest (Lowland) Northern one-third of the province. Coniferous forests, vast bogs and fens. High carbon storage in peatlands. Moose, Black Bears. Cold, long winters; moderate precipitation; thin, acidic Podzol soils; poor drainage.
Athabasca Plain Far northern, remote region near Lake Athabasca (part of Canadian Shield). Sparse forest, vast exposed lichen, specialized plants of the Athabasca Sand Dunes. Caribou. Extremely cold climate, vast sand deposits, thin, nutrient-poor Brunisolic/Cryosol soils.
Mid-Boreal Upland Interior of the Boreal region (higher elevation areas, better drained). Mixed coniferous/deciduous forest (Aspen, Pine, Fir). Better drainage supports large mammals (Elk, Deer). Moderate precipitation, better drainage than Boreal Lowlands, deep, well-developed Podzol soils.
Aspen Parkland Transitional band, central SK (e.g., around Saskatoon and Regina). A mix of fescue grasslands and small deciduous Aspen groves. High biodiversity and excellent wildlife habitat. Moderate climate (less extreme than the north/south); deep, highly fertile Chernozemic soils.
Grasslands Southern plains of the province (Mixed- and Short-Grass Prairie). Dominated by drought-tolerant grasses, home to keystone species like Prairie Dogs and endangered birds. Hot, dry summers; very cold winters; low annual precipitation; susceptible to wind erosion.

Geographical Context: Palliser's Triangle

The term Palliser’s Triangle refers to the semi-arid region in the southwest corner of the Canadian prairies, primarily encompassing Saskatchewan’s Grasslands biome.

  • Geographic Reality: This region receives significantly less rainfall (less than 350 mm annually) than the parkland and boreal areas, making it naturally prone to drought.
  • Historical Impact: Named after Captain John Palliser, who explored the area in the late 1850s and famously declared it too dry for traditional agriculture. This influential report initially discouraged settlement, although settlers later moved in, leading to the devastating Dust Bowl era when poor farming practices collided with the region’s natural climate limitations.
  • Biological Significance: It contains some of the last intact areas of native short- and mixed-grass prairie, supporting unique biodiversity that is highly adapted to arid conditions, including several endangered prairie species.

Understanding Palliser's report is key to grasping the historical challenges of soil erosion and water management in Saskatchewan.

The challenges explored in this unit—soil degradation, biodiversity loss, and resource use—are experienced differently across these major regions. For instance, soil salinization is a major problem in the Grasslands and Parkland due to historical agricultural practices, while forestry management is critical in the Boreal Forest and Mid-Boreal Upland regions.

In this expanded chapter, we will explore the essential and often undervalued components of our land-based systems, recognizing that their health and stability are directly tied to our own economic and ecological sustainability.

Section 1: The Essential Foundation: Soil Composition, Function, and Threats (ES20-TE1)

Why Soil Matters: The Pedosphere

Soil is often called the "skin of the Earth" because it is a thin, fragile layer that connects the abiotic (geosphere, atmosphere, hydrosphere) world with the biotic (biosphere) world. Soil formation is incredibly slow—it can take over 500 years to form just one inch of topsoil, making it an effectively non-renewable resource in the human timescale.

1.1 Soil Composition and Structure

Before soil can host life, it must be created. Soil begins as solid bedrock, which is slowly broken down by weathering into smaller fragments. This initial, foundational geological matter is called the parent material. The chemical composition and texture of this parent material determine the final characteristics of the soil, such as the proportions of sand, silt, and clay found in the deeper horizons.

The process of weathering involves three types of forces:

  • Physical Weathering: The mechanical breakdown of rock into smaller pieces (like sand and silt). Examples include the freeze-thaw cycle, where water enters rock cracks, freezes (expands), and pries the rock apart, or abrasion by wind and water.
  • Chemical Weathering: The alteration of a rock's chemical structure. Examples include hydrolysis (reaction with water) or oxidation (reaction with oxygen), which dissolve or chemically change the minerals, often releasing essential nutrients into the soil solution.
  • Biological Weathering: The breakdown of rock by living organisms. Examples include plant roots growing into cracks and exerting pressure, or lichens and mosses secreting organic acids that chemically dissolve the rock surface.

It is important to differentiate two key processes. Decomposition is the biological process where organic matter (dead leaves, animal waste) is broken down by fungi, bacteria, and insects, releasing nutrients and creating humus. Deposition, conversely, is the geological process where eroded inorganic material (like clay or silt carried by wind or water) is laid down or settled in a new location, often forming the parent material of future soil layers. Both contribute to the soil system but operate on very different timescales and material types.

Soil is not just dirt; it's a dynamic, living system made up of four equally important components:

  • Mineral Matter (≈45%): These are the particles (sand, silt, and clay) created from the slow process of weathering bedrock over millennia. Sand particles are the largest, allowing water to drain quickly. Clay particles are the smallest and stickiest, retaining water and nutrients but often making drainage difficult. The specific mix of these particles determines the soil texture.
  • Organic Matter (≈5%): This is the decaying remains of plants, animals, and microbes, known as humus in its stable form. This small component is crucial for soil fertility, structure (it acts like a glue to bind mineral particles together), and is the engine for nutrient recycling. High organic matter gives fertile prairie soils their rich, dark color and helps them hold twice their weight in water.
  • Soil Water (≈25%): Water is held in the tiny pore spaces between the mineral and organic components. This water is vital because it dissolves nutrients (making the ions available for plant absorption) and facilitates the chemical reactions necessary for life, soil formation, and decomposition. Without it, the flow of nutrients stops.
  • Soil Air (≈25%): When soil is not saturated with water, the remaining pore spaces are filled with air. This air is essential because it provides oxygen for the roots of plants and for the billions of microorganisms and decomposers living beneath the surface. Without soil air, anaerobic (oxygen-free) conditions take over, killing beneficial organisms and harming plant growth.

Soil Horizons and Soil Profiles

As soil forms over time, it naturally separates into distinct layers called soil horizons. A vertical cross-section of these layers, from the surface down to the bedrock, is known as the soil profile. Each horizon has a unique colour, texture, and composition due to the movement of water and materials through the profile, a process called leaching.

  • O-Horizon (Organic): This is the uppermost layer, composed entirely of organic material. It's often called topsoil along with the A-Horizon. The 'O' stands for organic: it contains forest litter, decomposing leaves, and humus. This layer is rich in nutrients and holds water exceptionally well, making it vital for all plant life. In the Boreal Forest, this layer is often very deep (peat).
  • A-Horizon (Topsoil/Mineral): A mix of mineral matter and decayed organic matter (humus) that gives it a dark colour. This is the layer where plant roots are most concentrated and is the primary zone of agricultural production.
  • E-Horizon (Eluviated): A light-coloured layer found below the A-Horizon, primarily in older, forested soils (like Podzols). 'E' stands for eluviated, meaning materials (clay, iron oxides, organic acids) have been extensively leached out and carried downward by water.
  • B-Horizon (Subsoil): Known as the zone of accumulation (or illuviation). Materials leached from the E or A horizons are deposited here. It is denser, lower in organic matter, and often reddish or yellowish due to the accumulation of clay and iron compounds.
  • C-Horizon (Parent Material): The layer directly beneath the soil profile, consisting of the unconsolidated material (like glacial till or river deposits) that the soil formed from. It is largely unaffected by biological activity.
  • R-Horizon (Bedrock): The lowest layer, consisting of the unweathered, solid bedrock that underlies the soil profile.

Career Profile: Agronomist

The science of soil management and crop production.

  • Role Overview: Agronomists are scientists who specialize in improving soil health, maximizing crop yields, and managing the environmental impact of agriculture. They advise farmers on crop rotation, pest management, and the efficient use of water and fertilizer.
  • Education & Skills: A university degree (B.Sc. or M.Sc.) in Agronomy, Soil Science, or Environmental Science is required. Critical skills include soil testing, data analysis, chemistry, and strong communication for consulting with farmers.
  • Link to ES20-TE1: This career directly applies knowledge of nutrient cycles, preventing salinization, minimizing erosion, and utilizing sustainable practices to ensure the long-term health of our Chernozemic soils.

Review: Nitrogen and Phosphorus Cycles

1.2 Soil Nutrient and Biogeochemical Cycling

Soil acts as the primary reservoir or medium for the cycling of the planet's most critical elements, all of which are interconnected with the atmosphere and hydrosphere:

  • Carbon Cycle: Soil is the largest terrestrial carbon reservoir. Plant decomposition and microbial activity release carbon to the atmosphere as CO2, while living plants pull CO2 from the atmosphere and transfer it to the soil via roots and detritus.
  • Nitrogen Cycle: Nitrogen (N2) gas, which makes up 78% of the atmosphere, is unusable by plants until soil bacteria convert it (through nitrogen fixation) into usable forms like nitrates. This process highlights the vital biotic link between the soil and the air.
  • Water (Hydrologic) Cycle: Soil and vegetation play a critical role in controlling the movement of water. Soil absorbs water via infiltration, storing it for plants and recharging groundwater reserves. Plants release water vapor back to the atmosphere through transpiration, influencing regional precipitation patterns.
  • Phosphorus Cycle: This cycle is unique because it has no significant atmospheric phase. Phosphorus is mainly found in rocks and sediments. Soil releases phosphorus through weathering, and plants absorb it as phosphate ions. Since it is easily bound to soil particles, the phosphorus cycle is often the slowest cycle, and human actions (like using mined fertilizers or causing erosion) heavily impact its availability in ecosystems.

The Role of Agriculture in Biogeochemical Cycles

Agricultural practices are essentially human-managed attempts to optimize these natural cycles to maximize food production. While modern farming has increased yields dramatically, it often creates imbalances, either enhancing a cycle (by adding nutrients) or inhibiting it (by interrupting natural processes).

  • Fertilization (Nitrogen and Phosphorus Cycles):

    The application of synthetic fertilizers is the most direct human alteration of the Nitrogen and Phosphorus cycles. While fertilizers instantly enhance nutrient availability for crop growth, excessive application often leads to nutrient runoff. This excess nitrogen and phosphorus then enters aquatic systems (rivers, lakes), driving eutrophication (algal blooms) and creating dead zones.

  • Irrigation (Water Cycle):

    In dry regions like the Grasslands, irrigation moves water from surface sources (rivers, reservoirs) or groundwater to the fields. This enhances the Water Cycle locally by increasing infiltration and transpiration. However, over-irrigation can lead to severe environmental inhibition, causing two major problems: groundwater depletion (where water is removed faster than it can be naturally recharged) and salinization (by raising the water table and drawing dissolved salts to the surface).

  • Crop Rotation (Nitrogen and Carbon Cycles):

    Crop rotation—planting different crops sequentially on the same land—is a powerful tool for sustainable agriculture. Planting legume crops (like peas, lentils, or clover) actively enhances the Nitrogen Cycle. These plants host nitrogen-fixing bacteria, naturally enriching the soil with nitrogen, reducing the need for synthetic fertilizers. This also indirectly enhances the Carbon Cycle by improving soil structure and organic matter retention.

  • Crop Selection (Carbon and Water Cycles):

    The choice of crop directly influences ecosystem services. Deep-rooted perennial crops (which live for multiple years) stabilize the soil better and sequester more carbon than annual crops (which must be replanted every year). Selecting drought-resistant crops in arid regions (like Palliser's Triangle) helps inhibit over-extraction from the Water Cycle by reducing the need for supplemental irrigation, making the practice more sustainable.

1.3 Major Threats to Soil Integrity in Saskatchewan

  1. Erosion: The removal of topsoil by wind and water, accelerated when protective plant cover (like native grasses or crop residue) is removed. The fertile A-horizon is lost first.

    Case Study: The 1930s Dust Bowl (The Dirty Thirties)

    The Dust Bowl is the most severe example of wind erosion in Canadian history, primarily impacting the Grasslands region of Saskatchewan. Farmers, following practices suitable for wetter climates, removed the native prairie grass cover and tilled the soil deeply (fallowing), leaving the land completely exposed. When a prolonged drought hit in the 1930s, strong winds lifted massive amounts of light, unprotected topsoil, creating black blizzards that carried dust for hundreds of kilometers. This event demonstrated the crucial protective role of deep-rooted native plants and led to the adoption of modern conservation techniques like shelterbelts and no-till farming.

  2. Salinization: The buildup of salts on the soil surface, primarily in low-lying areas. This occurs when high water tables bring dissolved salts to the surface, where the water evaporates, leaving the salts behind. Salinization significantly reduces crop yields.

    Case Study: Dryland Salinization in the Prairies

    The practice of summer fallowing (leaving fields bare for a year) was common in the Prairies to conserve moisture. However, without plants drawing up water, the water table rises close to the surface. As this water evaporates, it leaves behind dissolved mineral salts (like sodium, magnesium, and calcium) that were originally deep underground. This creates white, unproductive salt patches, often seen in the low-lying areas of Saskatchewan fields. The solution involves planting deep-rooted, perennial salt-tolerant crops like alfalfa or tall wheatgrass to actively lower the water table and draw the salts out of the root zone.

  3. Organic Matter Depletion: Continuous cropping without sufficient cover crops or manure slowly depletes the organic matter, reducing the soil’s ability to retain water and nutrients, and making it more susceptible to compaction.

    Case Study: The Long-Term Loss of Prairie Humus

    The deep, black Chernozemic soils of the Aspen Parkland and Grasslands were famed for their high initial organic matter content (up to 10%). However, after decades of intensive farming—especially relying on annual monocultures (like wheat)—soil organic matter has declined by 30% to 50% in many areas. This loss means the soil acts less like a sponge, leading to reduced water infiltration, increased compaction, and a greater reliance on synthetic fertilizers to maintain yields. Agronomists now stress the importance of practices like intercropping, cover cropping, and reintroducing perennial forages to slowly rebuild that vital organic carbon and protect the soil for future generations.

  4. Desertification: The process by which fertile land becomes desert, typically as a result of drought, deforestation, or inappropriate agriculture. While true deserts are rare in Saskatchewan, this process refers to the expansion of arid conditions into semi-arid areas, leading to severe land degradation.

    Case Study: The Great Sand Hills, Southwest Saskatchewan

    The Great Sand Hills, located near Leader, SK, are the largest active dune fields in the province. These dunes formed from ancient glacial meltwater deposits but were largely stabilized by native short- and mixed-grass prairie species over thousands of years. Early European settlement and overgrazing removed this stabilizing plant cover, particularly during periods of drought. This resulted in the active, uninhibited movement of sand, mimicking desertification. Today, this area is protected to allow native grasses to continue their work of stabilization, preventing the spread of active dunes into surrounding agricultural areas.
