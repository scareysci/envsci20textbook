Unit 3: Terrestrial Ecosystems – The Living Land

Introduction: Life on the Ground and the Biomes of Saskatchewan

When we think of Environmental Science, we often focus on global issues. However, the study of the land beneath our feet—our terrestrial ecosystems—is where the vast majority of human resource management occurs, especially here in Saskatchewan. These ecosystems are organized into major life zones called biomes.

A biome is a massive geographical area characterized by similar climate, geology, and typical plant and animal life. They are primarily determined by two abiotic factors: temperature and precipitation. Because Saskatchewan spans a wide range of latitude and climate zones, our province contains three major terrestrial biomes, each with unique ecological challenges and human uses.

Key Characteristics of Saskatchewan's Major Biomes

Biome/Ecoregion Location & Extent Key Biotic Characteristics Key Abiotic Characteristics
Boreal Forest (Lowland) Northern one-third of the province. Coniferous forests, vast bogs and fens. High carbon storage in peatlands. Moose, Black Bears. Cold, long winters; moderate precipitation; thin, acidic Podzol soils; poor drainage.
Athabasca Plain Far northern, remote region near Lake Athabasca (part of Canadian Shield). Sparse forest, vast exposed lichen, specialized plants of the Athabasca Sand Dunes. Caribou. Extremely cold climate, vast sand deposits, thin, nutrient-poor Brunisolic/Cryosol soils.
Mid-Boreal Upland Interior of the Boreal region (higher elevation areas, better drained). Mixed coniferous/deciduous forest (Aspen, Pine, Fir). Better drainage supports large mammals (Elk, Deer). Moderate precipitation, better drainage than Boreal Lowlands, deep, well-developed Podzol soils.
Aspen Parkland Transitional band, central SK (e.g., around Saskatoon and Regina). A mix of fescue grasslands and small deciduous Aspen groves. High biodiversity and excellent wildlife habitat. Moderate climate (less extreme than the north/south); deep, highly fertile Chernozemic soils.
Grasslands Southern plains of the province (Mixed- and Short-Grass Prairie). Dominated by drought-tolerant grasses, home to keystone species like Prairie Dogs and endangered birds. Hot, dry summers; very cold winters; low annual precipitation; susceptible to wind erosion.

Geographical Context: Palliser's Triangle

The term Palliser’s Triangle refers to the semi-arid region in the southwest corner of the Canadian prairies, primarily encompassing Saskatchewan’s Grasslands biome.

  • Geographic Reality: This region receives significantly less rainfall (less than 350 mm annually) than the parkland and boreal areas, making it naturally prone to drought.
  • Historical Impact: Named after Captain John Palliser, who explored the area in the late 1850s and famously declared it too dry for traditional agriculture. This influential report initially discouraged settlement, although settlers later moved in, leading to the devastating Dust Bowl era when poor farming practices collided with the region’s natural climate limitations.
  • Biological Significance: It contains some of the last intact areas of native short- and mixed-grass prairie, supporting unique biodiversity that is highly adapted to arid conditions, including several endangered prairie species.

Understanding Palliser's report is key to grasping the historical challenges of soil erosion and water management in Saskatchewan.

The challenges explored in this unit—soil degradation, biodiversity loss, and resource use—are experienced differently across these major regions. For instance, soil salinization is a major problem in the Grasslands and Parkland due to historical agricultural practices, while forestry management is critical in the Boreal Forest and Mid-Boreal Upland regions.

In this expanded chapter, we will explore the essential and often undervalued components of our land-based systems, recognizing that their health and stability are directly tied to our own economic and ecological sustainability.

Section 1: The Essential Foundation: Soil Composition, Function, and Threats (ES20-TE1)

Why Soil Matters: The Pedosphere

Soil is often called the "skin of the Earth" because it is a thin, fragile layer that connects the abiotic (geosphere, atmosphere, hydrosphere) world with the biotic (biosphere) world. Soil formation is incredibly slow—it can take over 500 years to form just one inch of topsoil, making it an effectively non-renewable resource in the human timescale.

1.1 Soil Composition and Structure

Healthy soil is a complex mixture of four primary components:

  1. Mineral Matter (45%): Sand, silt, and clay particles derived from weathered rock. The relative proportion of these three sizes determines the soil texture (e.g., loam, sandy clay).
  2. Organic Matter (5%): Decaying plant and animal material (humus) that gives fertile prairie soil its dark colour. Organic matter is crucial for binding soil particles, improving water retention, and providing nutrients.
  3. Soil Water (25%): Water held in the pore spaces, essential for dissolving nutrients and making them available to plants.
  4. Soil Air (25%):1.2 Soil Nutrient Cycling

    Soil is the primary medium for two crucial cycles:

    • Nitrogen Cycle: Bacteria in the soil convert atmospheric nitrogen (N2) into forms plants can use (nitrates and ammonium) through processes like nitrogen fixation and nitrification.
    • Carbon Cycle: Organic matter decomposition releases carbon back to the atmosphere as CO2, while vegetation pulls CO2 from the air and transfers it to the soil via roots and detritus. Soil is the largest terrestrial carbon reservoir.

    1.3 Major Threats to Soil Integrity in Saskatchewan

    1. Erosion: The removal of topsoil by wind and water, accelerated when protective plant cover (like native grasses or crop residue) is removed. The fertile A-horizon is lost first.
    2. Salinization: The buildup of salts on the soil surface, primarily in low-lying areas. This occurs when high water tables bring dissolved salts to the surface, where the water evaporates, leaving the salts behind. Salinization significantly reduces crop yields.
    3. Organic Matter Depletion: Continuous cropping without sufficient cover crops or manure slowly depletes the organic matter, reducing the soil’s ability to retain water and nutrients, and making it more susceptible to compaction.

Section 2: The Producers: Plants, Ecosystem Services, and Human Reliance (ES20-TE2)

Plants are the primary producers in almost all terrestrial ecosystems. They capture solar energy through photosynthesis, forming the energy base for the entire food web. Curriculum Outcome ES20-TE2 asks us to examine the critical role plants play, including the ways in which humans use them for economic and cultural benefit.

2.1 Ecosystem Services Provided by Plants

The value of native plants extends far beyond what we harvest:

  • Soil Stabilization: Native prairie grasses (e.g., Stipa and Bouteloua) have massive root systems that hold the soil together, dramatically reducing wind and water erosion.
  • Water Regulation: Deep roots improve the soil's infiltration rate, allowing rainwater to soak in rather than running off, which recharges groundwater supplies.
  • Carbon Sequestration: Forests and grasslands act as vital carbon sinks, pulling CO2 out of the atmosphere and storing the carbon in plant biomass and soil organic matter, mitigating climate change.

2.2 Human Reliance on Terrestrial Plant Resources

  1. Food and Fiber (Agriculture): Saskatchewan’s key crops (wheat, canola, pulse crops) feed the world. Managing these crops requires careful balancing of input (synthetic fertilizers, pesticides, water) and output (yield). This intensive management can sometimes conflict with ecosystem health, necessitating the shift toward sustainable and precision agriculture practices.
  2. Timber and Forestry: The Northern Boreal Forest is a critical source of lumber and pulp. Sustainable forestry involves managing harvest rates, ensuring adequate regeneration, and protecting old-growth areas that provide unique habitat and store maximum carbon. Clear-cutting, while efficient, can lead to immediate habitat loss and temporary increases in erosion.
  3. Medicines and Traditional Uses: Many plants native to our province have significant medicinal and cultural value, particularly within Indigenous communities. For instance, Sweetgrass (Hierochloe odorata) is used in ceremonies and relies on careful, traditional harvesting techniques that ensure the plant’s continued health (an example of sustainable harvest).

2.3 Spotlight: Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) and Indigenous Land Stewardship

Indigenous worldviews often emphasize the concept of reciprocity—a relationship where humans take only what they need and actively give back to the land, viewing themselves as part of the ecosystem, not separate from it.

Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) is the cumulative body of knowledge, practice, and belief, evolving by adaptive processes and handed down through generations, relating to the relationship of living beings (including humans) with their environment. TEK often includes practices such as:

  • Cultural Burning: Historically used in some regions to clear old brush, reduce dangerous fuel loads, and stimulate the growth of valuable forage plants, promoting ecosystem health and resilience.
  • Seasonal Rotation: Moving camps and harvesting areas to allow ecosystems time to recover, ensuring resources are available for future generations.

These practices embody a deep understanding of local ecology that is increasingly being integrated into modern environmental management plans across Canada.

Section 3: Biodiversity, Habitat Fragmentation, and Conservation (ES20-TE3)

3.1 The Need for Intact Habitat and Measuring Biodiversity

Curriculum Outcome ES20-TE3 focuses on recognizing the need for intact habitat to support animal populations and overall biodiversity. Biodiversity is the variety of life at three levels: genetic diversity, species diversity, and ecosystem diversity. A high level of biodiversity increases the resilience of an ecosystem—its ability to recover from disturbances like disease or climate change. In Saskatchewan, the rapid conversion of native prairie to agricultural land has been the primary driver of biodiversity decline.

3.2 Habitat Fragmentation and Edge Effects

Habitat Fragmentation occurs when large, continuous habitats are divided into smaller, isolated patches by roads, fields, or urban development.

  • Edge Effects: The boundary zones (edges) between fragmented habitats (e.g., between a forest patch and an agricultural field) experience different environmental conditions (more wind, sun, and predation) than the deep interior. This reduces the effective size of the habitat patch, negatively impacting species that need large, undisturbed areas.
  • Reduced Gene Flow: Fragmentation can prevent animal populations from mixing, leading to inbreeding and loss of genetic diversity, making the population more vulnerable to extinction.

Case Study: Prairie Species Under Pressure

  • The Burrowing Owl (Athene cunicularia): An endangered species relying entirely on native grasslands, nesting in burrows. Its decline is a direct indicator of habitat loss and fragmentation.
  • The Swift Fox (Vulpes velox): Once extirpated from Canada, this tiny fox relies on healthy, continuous short- and mixed-grass prairie. Reintroduction efforts depend heavily on managing land use and reducing human-caused mortality (like road deaths).
  • The Greater Sage Grouse (Centrocercus urophasianus): Highly sensitive to habitat disturbance, especially from human infrastructure (wells, roads). The male's elaborate courtship display requires large, open tracts of undisturbed land.

3.3 Conservation Strategies

Conservation efforts in Saskatchewan generally fall into two categories:

  1. In-situ Conservation (On-Site): Protecting species in their natural habitat. This includes creating Protected Areas (National Parks, Provincial Parks, Wildlife Refuges) and implementing Stewardship Agreements with landowners to protect critical habitats on private land.
  2. Ex-situ Conservation (Off-Site): Protecting species outside their natural habitat. This includes captive breeding programs (like those used for the Swift Fox) and seed banks, which store genetic material for future use.
